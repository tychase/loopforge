# Psychology Research Brief for Game Engagement

## Purpose

This project should use real psychology and game studies research to understand why games become engaging, satisfying, replayable, and motivating.

The goal is not to create exploitative addiction mechanics. The goal is to understand engagement deeply enough to design games that feel good, respect the player, and create strong short-session experiences.

## Key Research Areas

### 1. Self-Determination Theory

Self-Determination Theory is one of the most important frameworks for understanding intrinsic motivation.

It focuses on three psychological needs:

- Autonomy: the player feels agency and choice.
- Competence: the player feels themselves improving.
- Relatedness: the player feels connection to others, a world, a character, or a purpose.

Game design implications:

- Give the player meaningful choices.
- Make improvement visible.
- Give clear feedback.
- Let the player feel ownership over their path.
- Avoid systems that feel purely coercive or manipulative.

Questions for the agent:

- Does this mechanic increase player agency?
- Does this mechanic help the player feel skilled?
- Does this mechanic create a sense of connection or identity?
- Is the game fun because the player wants to continue, or because the system pressures them?

### 2. Flow State

Flow is the state where challenge and skill feel well matched. If the game is too easy, the player gets bored. If it is too hard, the player gets frustrated.

Game design implications:

- Start simple.
- Increase challenge gradually.
- Give immediate feedback.
- Keep goals clear.
- Avoid long downtime.
- Make failure feel fair and understandable.
- Let players recover from mistakes when possible.

Questions for the agent:

- Is the first 10 seconds too confusing?
- Does the player understand what to do?
- Is the difficulty curve smooth?
- Are deaths understandable?
- Does the player feel “I almost had it” after failing?

### 3. Reward Systems

Rewards can make games satisfying, but they can also become manipulative.

Healthy rewards:

- skill mastery
- visual feedback
- unlocks earned through play
- satisfying sounds
- meaningful upgrades
- new strategic options
- visible progress

Risky rewards:

- gambling-like random rewards
- rewards designed only to create compulsion
- dark-pattern retention tricks
- pressure timers
- fake scarcity
- endless grind with little agency

Game design implications:

- Use rewards to reinforce mastery and curiosity.
- Avoid designing around compulsion.
- Avoid gambling-like monetization logic.
- Do not use real-money mechanics.
- Keep the game short, satisfying, and respectful.

### 4. Variable Reward Schedules

Variable rewards can increase persistence because the reward timing is unpredictable. This is one reason slot machines and some gambling systems are powerful.

For this project, variable rewards should be used carefully and ethically.

Acceptable uses:

- randomized upgrade choices
- surprise enemy variants
- occasional rare visual effects
- unpredictable level modifiers
- non-monetary surprises

Avoid:

- real-money rewards
- loot-box style manipulation
- hidden odds
- systems designed to keep players stuck
- gambling-like loops

### 5. Feedback Loops

Games feel better when every action gives feedback.

Feedback types:

- movement response
- pickup sound
- particle burst
- score animation
- enemy hit flash
- screen shake
- upgrade animation
- progress bar
- end-of-run summary

Questions for the agent:

- Does the player know when they did something good?
- Does the player know why they failed?
- Does every action feel responsive?
- Are rewards visible and audible?
- Does the UI communicate progress clearly?

## Important Ethical Rule

The system should not optimize for addiction.

The system should optimize for:

- fun
- clarity
- mastery
- replayability
- agency
- curiosity
- satisfying feedback
- short-session delight

The Psychological Researcher Agent should flag any mechanic that feels exploitative.

Research starting points: Ryan, Rigby, and Przybylski applied Self-Determination Theory to computer game motivation; their framework centers needs like autonomy, competence, and relatedness. A later motivational model by Przybylski, Rigby, and Ryan also uses Self-Determination Theory to explain sustained game engagement and player well-being. For variable rewards, use caution: research on gambling-like reinforcement schedules shows that different reinforcement schedules can affect persistence and behavior.
